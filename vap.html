Protocol for Prevention of Ventilator-Associated Pneumonia

01

Definition

•Hospital-acquired pneumonia (HAP)
–Pneumonia that occurs ≥ 48 hours after admission and did not appear to be incubating at the time of admission
•Ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP)
–A type of HAP that develops ≥ 48 hours after initiation of invasive mechanical ventilation
•Ventilator-associated events (VAE)
–Sustained increase in ventilator settings (FiO₂ or PEEP) after a period of stable or decreasing support
–Only ~25–33% of VAEs are due to pneumonia; other causes include fluid overload, atelectasis, and ARDS
02

Indication

•Patients admitted to the intensive care unit receiving invasive mechanical ventilation via an endotracheal (oral or nasal) or tracheostomy tube, from initiation of ventilation until liberation from the ventilator
–Procedures may be limited if patients are in the prone position, have difficulty opening the mouth, or are hemodynamically unstable
03

Recommendations

1.General Care Principles

(1)Avoid intubation when possible
•Use noninvasive respiratory support modalities including non-invasive positive pressure ventilation or high-flow nasal cannula whenever safe and feasible, including the post-extubation period to prevent reintubation
•Assess daily for readiness to be liberated from ventilatory support as early as clinically appropriate
(2)Minimize sedation
•Keep patients under light sedation whenever possible
•Avoid benzodiazepines when possible
(3)Provide early exercise and mobilization
(4)Provide early enteral rather than parenteral nutrition
(5)Ventilator circuit management
•Change the ventilator circuit only if visibly soiled or malfunctioning
•Drain circuit condensate away from the patient

2.Preventing Aspiration

(1)Patient positioning
•Elevate the head of the bed to 30–45°, including during position changes
–Check for patient discomfort and risk of pressure injury
–Use appropriate tilt adjustment and bedding facilities
(2)Management of endotracheal tube
•Maintain endotracheal tube cuff pressure at minimal occlusive settings (20–30 cmH₂O)
•For patients expected to require mechanical ventilation for >48 hours, use an endotracheal tube with a subglottic suction port, if available (In our center: TaperGuard Evac, Medtronic)
•For patients intubated with a subglottic-suction-capable tube or appropriate tracheostomy tube, apply continuous subglottic secretion aspiration
(3)Suction oral secretions before each position change

3.Management of Oral Hygiene and Suction

(1)Oral hygiene
•Provide regular oral care at least 3 times per day (once per nursing shift)
•Toothbrushing is recommended when feasible
•Use of chlorhexidine is no longer recommended
(2)Suctioning
•Always perform hand hygiene before and after suction procedures; wear clean disposable gloves during procedures
•Use sterile saline (or sterile distilled water) for tube irrigation; replace with a new container each time
•Use separate catheters for oral suction and endotracheal suction
•Use one suction collection bottle per patient. For continued use, empty at least once daily
